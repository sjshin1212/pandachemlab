Research Background

üéØ Industrial Importance

Furfuryl alcohol is a crucial molecule for synthesizing furan resins, widely used in coatings and adhesives due to their excellent heat stability and chemical resistance. The challenge lies in selective hydrogenation‚Äîproducing the desired furfuryl alcohol while avoiding unwanted byproducts.

‚öóÔ∏è The Challenge

During furfural hydrogenation, if the process breaks carbon-carbon bonds instead of selectively hydrogenating the carbonyl group, undesirable compounds like furan and tetrahydrofuran (THF) are produced, reducing the efficiency of industrial processes.

üî¨ Our Approach

We used advanced computational chemistry methods to understand exactly how these unwanted byproducts form at the atomic level, comparing traditional platinum catalysts with promising platinum-tin alloy surfaces.

Research Goal

Determine Optimal Reaction Pathways

Find out exactly how undesirable products form by determining the most likely reaction pathway on an atomic scale. This understanding could help optimize surface alloys as more effective catalysts for industrial furfuryl alcohol production.

Computational Methods

VASP Computer Modeling Simulations

We used Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) to model starting and ending molecules for each possible reaction. VASP adjusts each structure to find the lowest energy state using density functional theory (DFT).

NEB Simulation for Reaction Pathways

Using Nudged Elastic Band (NEB) simulations, we modeled how atoms move and bonds break or form during reactions. This provided energy profiles showing activation energies for each pathway.

Selection Criteria: Reactions with lower activation energy are more likely to occur, so we identified the most favorable pathways based on these calculations.

Surface Comparison Analysis

We systematically compared reaction behaviors on pure Pt(111) surfaces versus Platinum-Tin (PtSn) alloy surfaces to understand how the addition of tin atoms affects reaction selectivity.

Focus Areas: Hydrogen binding energy, surface reactivity, and activation energy barriers for different reaction pathways.

Key Findings & Results

Pt(111) Surface - High Byproduct Formation

High H Binding Energy

Pt(111) surface showing byproduct formation

On pure platinum surfaces, the strong hydrogen binding facilitates unwanted carbon-carbon bond breaking, leading to byproduct formation.

  • Formation of CO byproducts is thermodynamically favorable
  • Byproducts readily form after first-step hydrogenation
  • Lower activation barriers for undesired pathways

PtSn Alloy Surface - Selective Production

Optimized H Binding

PtSn alloy surface promoting selective hydrogenation

Tin atoms modify nearby platinum behavior by donating electrons, creating optimal conditions for selective furfuryl alcohol production.

  • CO byproduct formation is thermodynamically unfavorable
  • Carbonyl group readily hydrogenated due to Sn presence
  • Significant energy barriers (~2 eV) prevent unwanted reactions

Key Mechanistic Insight

Electronic Effect of Tin

Sn atoms donate electrons to nearby Pt atoms, making it harder for hydrogen atoms to bind strongly to the surface. This prevents excessive hydrogen accumulation near active sites, which helps prevent unwanted hydrogenation reactions that break carbon-carbon bonds.

H-O-H molecular interactions

Selective Pathway Enhancement

The modified electronic structure creates an environment where carbonyl group hydrogenation is kinetically and thermodynamically favorable, while ring hydrogenation and C-C bond breaking face significant energy barriers.

Research Conclusion

PtSn alloy surfaces demonstrate superior selectivity for furfuryl alcohol production compared to pure platinum. The energy barrier for undesired C-C bond breaking is relatively low on Pt(111) surfaces, aided by surface hydrogen. However, on PtSn surfaces, tin introduction creates significant activation barriers (~2 eV), while the electronic modification enhances selective carbonyl hydrogenation.
