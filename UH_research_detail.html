Project Summary

Organobismuth compounds are emerging as promising alternatives to **traditional transition-metal catalysts** due to Bismuth's lower toxicity, affordability, and environmental friendliness. My research uses **quantum mechanical models (Hartree-Fock and DFT)** and **quasi atomic orbital (QUAO) analysis** to study how ligands affect the stability of the crucial Bismuth-Hydrogen (Bi-H) bond, guiding the design of safer, more efficient catalysts.

Problem & Motivation

üí∏ Cost and Toxicity of Transition Metals

Transition metals (like Platinum, Palladium, Rhodium) are essential for chemical reactions like hydrogenation and cross-coupling. However, they are **expensive**, **toxic**, and **environmentally hazardous**, limiting sustainable and large-scale applications.

Transition metals catalyze different reaction pathways such as hydrogenation and cross-coupling.

The majority of high-level chemistry often focuses on transition metals.

‚ú® Bismuth as a Greener Alternative

Bismuth, a main-group element, is **safer** (often used for medicinal purposes), **cheaper** (costing significantly less per kg than Pt, Pd, or Rh), and capable of mediating **redox catalysis** under mild conditions. Developing organobismuth catalysts can significantly improve sustainable chemistry.

Research Question & Goal

How to minimize bond dissociation energy for homolytic cleavage of Bi-H?

The instability of the bismuth-hydrogen (Bi-H) bond limits the use of organobismuth complexes. Homolytic cleavage of the Bi-H bond (breaking it into radicals) is essential for the catalyst to cycle efficiently between the Bi(III) and Bi(II) oxidation states.

Homolytic cleavage of the Bi-H bond to generate Bi(II) radicals.

Goal: Rational Catalyst Design

By clarifying how **ligands** (molecules bound to the bismuth center) affect the Bi-H bond and overall stability, the research aims to guide the **rational design** of organobismuth catalysts for applications like hydrogenation and radical polymerization.

Computational Methodology

Flow chart of the computational analysis process.

Geometry Optimization (Method #1)

Molecular structures are optimized using **quantum mechanical methods (Hartree-Fock and Density Functional Theory, DFT)**. This confirms the **true local minima** on the Potential Energy Surface (PES) by running frequency calculations (checking for imaginary frequency values).

Frequency calculation output: Imaginary modes indicate the optimized point is not a true local minimum.

Quasi-Atomic Orbital (QUAO) Analysis (Method #2)

Bonding is studied using **QUAO analysis**, which calculates **Kinetic Bond Order (KBO)** and **Density Bond Order (DBO)**. This provides intrinsic bonding information, including electron occupation and s/p-character, crucial for determining stability.

Density Bond Orders (DBO) and Kinetic Bond Orders (KBO) derived from QUAO.

Independent Variables and Ligand Design

The stability is systematically studied across three **independent variables** related to **ligand design**:

  • 1. Substituent Types: Varying electron-donating/withdrawing groups (-F, -NH2, -NO2, etc.) on the phenyl rings.
  • 2. Substituent Positions: Comparing para vs. ortho positions on the phenyl rings.
  • 3. Denticity: Exploring the effect of a sulfur bridge between phenyl groups (tetradentate ligand).

Types and positions of substituents on the phenyl rings affect the bonding profiles of organobismuth hydrides.

QUAO Bonding Analysis Sample

Analyzing Bonding Orbitals

By comparing the QUAO profiles and the calculated bond dissociation energies, we can identify which substituent types and positions lead to the most stable and efficient organobismuth catalysts.

Conclusion & Impact

Impact: Greener, Safer Chemistry

If successful, this research will clarify how to stabilize and tune Bi-H bonds, helping chemists design more efficient **organobismuth catalysts**. This provides a pathway to reduce reliance on **hazardous transition metals** and expands the toolkit for sustainable catalysis in hydrogenation, radical polymerization, and beyond.

This is one step toward greener, safer, and more affordable chemistry.
