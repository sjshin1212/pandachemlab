Research Goal

By combining structural modeling with bonding analysis, my research aims to guide the design of safer, more efficient bismuth-based catalysts.

**Focus:** Accelerating the identification of Nobel, stable bonding motifs.

Background and Research Question

The Challenge and the Bismuth Alternative

Transition Metal Limitations

Many transition-metal catalysts are expensive, toxic, and not environmentally-friendly. For example, many transition metals such as palladium have shown to have detrimental effects on overall human health. Such factors limit sustainable use and poses challenges for green chemistry applications.

The high cost of Platinum, Palladium, and Rhodium per kg.

Bismuth: A Sustainable Alternative

Bismuth, one of the main-group elements, offers an especially promising alternative to transition metal catalysts. The safety of bismuth is evidenced by its widespread use in pharmaceutical ingredients such as bismuth subsalicylate. Additionally, bismuth is cheaper than many transition metals. Organobismuth compounds are emerging as promising alternatives to traditional transition-metal catalysts.

Price comparison including Bismuth and the structure of bismuth subsalicylate.

Research Focus and Question

Focusing on Bond Cleavage

The generation of reactive Bi-centered radicals through homolytic Bi-ligand bond cleavage is worth noting due to its ability to initiate various transformations. Consequently, many contemporary researches emphasize the design of optimal ligand architectures to stabilize the molecular complex and suppress undesired side reactions. Electron-donating substituents enhance radical delocalization.

Homolytic cleavage of the Bi-H bond and Bismuth's redox catalysis pathways.

Primary Research Question

How do different substituents (F, $\text{NH}_2$, $\text{OCH}_3$, $\text{CH}_3$, $\text{SCH}_3$, $\text{NO}_2$), their positions on the aryl rings, and the presence of bridging groups ($\text{CH}_2$, S, $\text{SO}_2$) influence the electronic structure, quasi-atomic orbital (QUAO) profiles, and homolytic bond dissociation energies in organobismuth complexes?

Computational Methodology

The flow of computational analysis showing optimization, QUAO, and BDE calculation steps.

Geometry Optimization & Structure Selection

All computations will be performed with the ORCA 6.0 program package. Geometry optimizations will be performed in benzene solvent with $\text{def2-TZVP}$ and $\text{def2-SVPD}$ basis sets. The $\text{M06-L/def2-TZVP/ECP/CPCM}$ level of theory will be primarily used for all geometry optimizations. Analytic vibrational frequency analyses will be performed to confirm that the optimized geometries correspond to true ground-state minima.

Initial molecular complexes screened based on favorable bond dissociation energies.

Fundamental $\text{Bi(di-aryl)SPh}$ framework for DFT screening, where X and Y are varied.

Bonding Analysis (QUAO) and BDE Calculation

Bonding will be analyzed using quasi-atomic orbital (QUAO) analysis. QUAO provides insight into electron density distribution and bond character. This analysis enables the assessment of bond strengths, radical character, and electronic delocalization. The homolytic bond dissociation energies (BDE) of the Bi-SPh bonds will be determined from single-point energy evaluations on the optimized single doublet states.

Comparison of single-point energy magnitudes for different auxiliary basis set configurations.

QUAO Descriptors and Independent Variables

The resulting QUAO data will provide quantitative insight into the distribution of electron density, hybridization character at metal center, and degree of orbital delocalization. These are the **dependent variables** of the study. We are planning on varying the following three **independent variables**: substituent types (F, $\text{NH}_2$, $\text{OCH}_3$, $\text{CH}_3$, $\text{SCH}_3$, $\text{NO}_2$), substituent positions (para vs. ortho), and the denticity of ligands.

Visualization of the electronic interaction between quasi-atomic orbitals.

Investigated Variables and Structures

1. Substituent Type and Position

We will systematically introduce substituent groups which maximize radical delocalization and steric protection. The current list of substituents are F, $\text{NH}_2$, $\text{OCH}_3$, $\text{CH}_3$, $\text{SCH}_3$, $\text{NO}_2$. Positions include para versus ortho on phenyl groups.

Model structure featuring electron-withdrawing $\text{NO}_2$ substituents.

2. Bridging Group Presence (Y in Figure 2)

The presence of bridging groups ($\text{CH}_2$, S, $\text{SO}_2$) influence the electronic distribution and steric environment of the complexes. For the sulfur- and methyl-bridged analogues, substituents will be restricted to the para positions to minimize steric interference.

Model structure featuring $\text{SCH}_3$ substituents for analysis.

3. Ligand Denticity (Tetradentate)

Ligand denticity (e.g., tridentate, tetradentate) is an independent variable. Tetradentate ligands donate four pairs of electrons to the central bismuth atom. Sterically demanding and chelating ligands protect the metal center.

A complex stabilized by bulky $\text{tBu}$ ligands, highlighting steric protection.

Green and Sustainable Chemistry

By enabling the rational design of non-toxic, earth-abundant main-group catalysts, the research supports transition away from precious and hazardous transition metals. Developing organobismuth catalysts could significantly improve sustainable and efficient catalyst use.

Predictive Computational Toolkit

If a clear statistical correlation is established between BDE and QUAO profiles, the computationally inexpensive QUAO analysis could serve as a practical substitute for the more computationally intensive BDE calculations in future studies. Such correlations would not only streamline computational workflows but also provide more orbital-level understanding of bond stability.

Refining Catalytic Mechanisms

QUAO-based descriptors may reveal how electronic delocalization within $\pi$-conjugated or electron-donating ligands governs spin distribution and suppresses unwanted radical recombination or dimerization. Such insights could refine the general understanding of radical formation and stabilization in organobismuth systems.

Research Conclusion

The outcome of this study will contribute to a transferable framework for interpreting Bi-X bond activation across diverse ligand environments, advancing greener chemistry.
