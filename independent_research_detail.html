Research Overview (Abstract)

The mechanical properties of biodegradable plastics often fall short compared to traditional plastics, presenting significant challenges in balancing biodegradability with performance. While traditional plastics like polyethylene (PE) and polyethylene terephthalate (PET) offer superior mechanical and thermal properties, many biodegradable alternatives struggle to match these attributes. Copolymer blends, such as PLA-PHB, offer a promising approach to reconcile this trade-off. This exploration underscores the ongoing challenge of balancing environmental sustainability with the need for high-performance materials.

Problem Background

üéØ The Chronic Problem with Plastics

Petroleum-based plastics like PE and PET do not degrade naturally, leading to a global crisis of persistent plastic waste in marine and soil ecosystems. The issues stem from the inherent structure of the polymer chains, which resist degradation regardless of whether they are biobased or petroleum-based.

Biodegradable plastics face a critical trade-off between performance and degradation. For example, Poly(lactic acid) (PLA) has favorable properties but slow degradation. Conversely, Polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) offers excellent biodegradability but poor mechanical performance.

Key Degradation Steps

  • Abiotic: Industrial process (e.g., photolysis, photothermal) to break long chains into oligomers.
  • Biotic: Microbial enzymes break down polymers into smaller molecules (oligomers, dimers, monomers), followed by assimilation and mineralization.

Structural Characteristics & Properties

Crystallinity vs. Degradability

The degree of crystallinity is a critical factor determining a plastic's properties. Increasing crystallization enhances mechanical and thermal properties (e.g., stabilization of specific volume change). However, high crystallinity significantly hinders both microbial and industrial depolymerization processes, thus decreasing biodegradability.

Figure 2: Effects of degree of crystallization on specific volume

Molecular Structures & Thermal Properties

Molecular structure (monomer unit structure) determines durability and resistance to thermal deformation. Presence of bulky groups or aromatic rings strengthens the intermolecular force. For biodegradable plastics, functional groups like ketone, ether, or ester induce stronger intermolecular forces.

The methyl groups ($\text{CH}_3$) in the backbone of PLA and PHB increase steric hindrance, reducing chain mobility and contributing to better mechanical/thermal properties.

Chemical Additives

The most desirable properties are stiffness, brittleness, high glass transition temperature ($T_g$), and high melting temperature ($T_m$). Additives like cyclic aliphatic or aryl groups are effective in increasing structural rigidity, which enhances $T_g$.

Natural Fiber Composites

Forming composites is a viable way to reinforce strength, focusing on natural fibers and biobased plastics for biodegradability. The cellulose in natural fibers contains hydroxyl groups that form hydrogen bonds to strengthen the composite structure.

Hemp fabric/PHB composite is an example, showing comparable strength and stiffness to structural lumber while maintaining a decent level of biodegradability.

Copolymer Technique

PLA-PHB blends are a notable solution to reinforce the flaws of both polymers. PHB crystals act as a nucleating agent in PLA, significantly enhancing the mechanical properties of PLA while successfully maintaining biodegradability.

This technique directly addresses the quintessential challenge of balancing performance (PLA's strength) with environmental benefit (PHB's biodegradability).

Potential Future Research

Future research should focus on optimizing these techniques and integrating biomaterials such as starch. The goal is to enhance biodegradability further while devising effective methods to maintain the favorable mechanical and thermal properties achieved by structural modifications.
