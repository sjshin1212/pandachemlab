Project Summary

Organobismuth compounds are emerging as promising alternatives to traditional transition-metal catalysts due to Bismuth's lower toxicity, affordability, and environmental friendliness. My research uses quantum mechanical models (Hartree-Fock and DFT) and quasi atomic orbital (QUAO) analysis to study how ligands affect the stability of the crucial Bismuth-Hydrogen (Bi-H) bond, guiding the design of safer, more efficient catalysts.

Problem & Motivation

üí∏ Cost and Toxicity of Transition Metals

Transition metals are widely used in reactions like hydrogenation and cross-coupling due to their excellent ability to undergo redox cycles. However, many are expensive, toxic, and environmentally hazardous, posing challenges for green chemistry applications. Bismuth offers a promising alternative, being perceived as one of the safest metals often employed for medicinal purposes.

The Bismuth Advantage
  • Bismuth is safer and more affordable compared to Platinum, Palladium, and Rhodium.
  • It is capable of mediating redox catalysis under mild conditions.
  • Developing organobismuth catalysts could significantly improve sustainable and efficient catalyst use.

Transition metals catalyze different reaction pathways such as hydrogenation and cross-coupling.

Research Question & Goal

How to minimize bond dissociation energy for homolytic cleavage of Bi-H?

The instability of the bismuth-hydrogen (Bi-H) bond limits the use of organobismuth complexes. Homolytic cleavage of the Bi-H bond (breaking it into radicals) is essential for the catalyst to cycle efficiently between the Bi(III) and Bi(II) oxidation states.

Homolytic cleavage of the Bi-H bond to generate Bi(II) radicals.

Challenge: Ligand Effect on Stability

Mechanistic pathways and ligand effects on Bi-H bond stability remain underexplored. Current literature suggests the Bi(III)/Bi(I) redox cycle, which involves homolytic cleavage, is primarily affected by the ligand design.

Goal: By clarifying how ligands (molecules bound to the bismuth center) affect the Bi-H bond and overall stability, the research aims to guide the rational design of organobismuth catalysts for applications like hydrogenation and radical polymerization.

Computational Methodology

Flow chart of the computational analysis process.

Geometry Optimization (Method #1)

Molecular structures are optimized using quantum mechanical methods (Hartree-Fock and DFT). This process is necessary to confirm true local minima on the Potential Energy Surface (PES) by running frequency calculations (checking for imaginary frequency values).

Frequency calculation output: Imaginary modes indicate the optimized point is not a true local minimum.

Quasi-Atomic Orbital (QUAO) Analysis (Method #2)

Bonding is analyzed using QUAO analysis, focusing on Kinetic Bond Order (KBO), Density Bond Order (DBO), orbital occupation, and s/p-character. This provides intrinsic bonding information crucial for determining stability.

Density Bond Orders (DBO) and Kinetic Bond Orders (KBO) derived from QUAO.

Independent Variables and Ligand Design

The stability is systematically studied across three independent variables related to ligand design:

  • 1. Substituent Types: Varying electron-donating/withdrawing groups (e.g., -F, -NH2, -OCH3, -NO2) on the phenyl rings.
  • 2. Substituent Positions: Comparing para versus ortho positions on the phenyl rings.
  • 3. Denticity: Exploring the effect of a sulfur bridge between phenyl groups (e.g., tetradentate ligands).

Types and positions of substituents on the phenyl rings affect the bonding profiles of organobismuth hydrides.

Analyzing Bonding Orbitals

By comparing the QUAO profiles and the calculated bond dissociation energies across these variations, the research aims to identify which substituent leads to the most stable and efficient organobismuth catalysts.

Conclusion & Impact

Impact: Greener, Safer Chemistry

The insights gained will clarify how efficiently the catalyst can cycle between Bi(III) and Bi(II) oxidation states. If successful, this research will help chemists design more efficient organobismuth catalysts, reducing reliance on hazardous transition metals and expanding the toolkit for sustainable catalysis.

This is one step toward greener, safer, and more affordable chemistry.
